Research interests

Inductive inference, Ockham's razor, formal epistemology, learning theory, belief revision, topology, statistics and machine learning.

My PhD project investigates what can be learned from statistical data. It develops a mathematical framework in which to investigate a hierarchy of learnability concepts and characterize precisely when they are achievable. It is motiviated by a feasibility-contextualism that mantains that epistemic justification consists in achieving the best learning performance feasible in a problem context. The project also give a precise notion of simplicity for statistical hypotheses, and provides a non-circular epistemic justification for Ockham's razor. My work builds on the learning-theoretic framework developed by E. Mark Gold and Hilary Putnam, and its topological development by my dissertation advisor Kevin T. Kelly. By lifting the insights of algorithmic learning theory to statistical data, we establish a perspicuous topological framework in which to study real-life empirical inquiry as it is practiced in machine learning and statistics.
